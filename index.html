Cycling in North Negros, Philippines

Historical Silay City

The main town where old pre-war colonial houses abound, this Silay - Airport road is the entrance into the Guimbalaon and Lantawan corridor leading to Patag, a mountain village where the last of the Japanese army held out during the second world war. It’s 29 kilometres to the summit so make sure to go at just the right pace to have some gas left in the tank for the challenging climbs starting from Guimbalaon, about 20 kilometres from El Ideal—the oldest cafe in Negros, open for business since 1920.

Taking the High Road

The penultimate climb on the road to Lantawan, this section will challenge even the toughest of climbers. Make sure you have the right gearing and enough power and energy in the legs to take this zigzagging wall of a climb. Be careful of incoming vehicular traffic if you opt to weave due to the steep blind corner. Not an ordinary climb for the novice rider but persevere and you will be rewarded with a most expansive, most beautiful vista of the western coast of North Negros and the surrounding coastal views.

Lantawan

This century-old mahogany welcomes riders to the hilltop village of Lantawan above Silay with sweeping views of Bacolod to the southwest, and Victorias to the northwest. This tree will usher brave souls an eery welcome if you’re willing to climb up the winding road with an incredible rise of 15%. Respect the gatekeepers and the beautiful natural environment with a respectful nod and a “tabi” - the local language greeting for the spirits guarding the sacred mountains.

Stairway to Heaven

The last climb on the connecting road between the two mountain villages of Lantawan and Patag, this road could easily qualify as the hardest climb in the Philippines. Nestled in the heart of the North Negros Natural Park System which comprises 85,000 hectares encompassing a number of municipalities in Negros Occidental province, this hidden stretch of road will be a challenge of the highest caliber in terms of difficulty, but can also be one of the most beautiful roads to ride a bike on.

Last Flat Road

This overflow bridge is the last flat section right before the road turns to the sky at kilometre 16 on the road to Gawahon. From here, expect to work all the way up to the welcome sign and if you’re brave enough, all the way to the first falls at the end of the road 10 kms from here.

Above the Clouds

On a clear day, on this plateau which is just a kilometre before you arrive at the “Welcome to Gawahon” sign, will allow riders unobstructed views across the channel to see Isla de Gigantes and Pan de Azucar on the Northern Panay horizon.

Accessible right from the end of the concrete road, this is the first falls of seven in the park. Cyclists who succeed in slaying this difficult climb will have a refreshing reward. Hike up the trail for about half an hour more and you will find the Fifth and Sixth Falls consisting of the highest drops in the series and where the water breaks into twin falls.

Seven Falls at Gawahon

Road to Gawahon

With 26 kilometres from the town of Victorias to the first waterfalls of Gawahon, the road is relatively flat for the first 5 kilometres allowing for a long warm up. Seven kilometres in will show an average gradient of 2%, gradually increasing until kilometre 14 right after the village of Estado, where the road begins to gradually rise to around 5% - 7%. After the overflow bridge, the real work begins at kilometre 16 where the road turns straight up, all the way to the mountain resort of Gawahon, climbing steadily for the next 8 kms until you hit the welcome arch. Those that have some gas left in the tank can attempt to go straight to the first falls with uphill climbs under the cover of the canopy of trees. Take care and mind the slippery patches of moss on the damp and uneven road surfaces approaching the entry to the park leading all the way to the first waterfalls. This is an extremely challenging climb with a rewarding waterfall access for willing riders.
